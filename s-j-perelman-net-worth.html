S. J. Perelman Overview and Wiki

Early Life and Education

Sidney Joseph Perelman, born in Brooklyn, New York, embarked on a journey that would make him a legendary figure in American humor. His early life in New York City, coupled with his education at Brown University (though he didn’t graduate), laid the foundation for his unique literary style.

Perelman’s Jewish heritage and upbringing in a family that frequently moved due to business failures also influenced his worldview and humor.

Early Career and Writing Style

Perelman’s journey began in the 1920s after dropping out of Brown University and moving to Greenwich Village in Manhattan, New York City. He started as a cartoonist and humorist, contributing to various magazines.

His early works were characterized by their brevity and humor, often infused with a sense of ridicule, irony, and wryness. Perelman’s unique style was evident in his numerous brief sketches, which he often referred to as feuilletons — a term denoting literary or scientific articles and serial stories.

Contributions to The New Yorker

He is best known for his humorous short pieces written for The New Yorker over many years. These pieces, often considered the first examples of surrealist humor in the United States, were a direct assault on the cultural and commercial society of the time.

His work in The New Yorker was not just about making people laugh; it was about challenging the status quo and exposing the absurdities of everyday life.

Hollywood and Broadway Success

In addition to his magazine work, Perelman’s career in Hollywood and Broadway was equally illustrious. He co-wrote scripts for the Marx Brothers’ films Monkey Business (1931) and Horse Feathers (1932).

His most significant achievement in cinema came with the Academy Award-winning screenplay for Around the World in 80 Days (1956).

On Broadway, he wrote at least five original plays from 1932 to 1963, including collaborations with his wife, Laura West. His works on Broadway showcased his versatility and ability to engage audiences in different formats.

Legacy and Influence

Perelman’s influence extended beyond his lifetime, impacting future generations of writers and comedians. His use of wordplay, obscure references, and a unique sense of the ridiculous set him apart from his contemporaries.

He was heavily influenced by authors like James Joyce and Flann O’Brien, yet his style remained precise, clear, and distinctly his own. Perelman’s legacy as a pioneer in American comedy and satire continues to be celebrated, with his works serving as a reference point for humorists and writers globally.

FAQs about S.J. Perelman

Who was S.J. Perelman?

S.J. Perelman, born Sidney Joseph Perelman, was an American humorist and screenwriter known for his humorous short pieces in The New Yorker and his screenwriting, including for the Marx Brothers and the Academy Award-winning Around the World in 80 Days.

What were Perelman’s notable works?

He is famous for writing the Marx Brothers films Monkey Business and Horse Feathers, as well as for Around the World in 80 Days. He also authored several books and plays.

What was Perelman’s writing style?

He was known for his audacious and original style, featuring wordplay, satire, and parodies of popular culture and literary forms.

Did he write plays for Broadway?

Yes, he wrote at least five original plays produced on Broadway between 1932 and 1963.

What were some of Perelman’s influences and contributions to comedy?

He influenced many humorists, including Woody Allen. His work has been praised by Garrison Keillor and Dylan Moran, among others.
