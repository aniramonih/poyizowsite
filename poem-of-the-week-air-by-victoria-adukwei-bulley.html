Poem of the week: Air by Victoria Adukwei Bulley

Posted by Billy Koelling on Tuesday, August 20, 2024
Carol Rumens's poem of the weekPoetry

Moving elusively between private and public worlds, the poet finds grace in small, shared moments

Air

Friend, I saw you sitting
at the window of yourself

high up in the loft
watching it all go on without you.

I was ice & you were almost shadow.
I waved like a child on a passing boat.

Unsure of whether or not this counts
I want us to remember the unreadable air.

How it waits to be recast
by the touch of our lungs -

our breath alone,
a most humble poem:

in, out, the couplet we write
without thinking. Don’t go.

We must stay alive
to our place in the family

of green & breathing things
that use even our sighs

to make sweetness from light.
What grace. Allow what is simple

to be simple. Accept it as
truth. Quiet as it’s kept
we help the trees to breathe too.

Victoria Adukwei Bulley is a writer and film-maker of Ghanaian heritage, born and raised in Essex, England, and an alumna of the thriving artistic development project Barbican Young Poets. Published this month, her first full-length collection, Quiet, moves between anger and tenderness, scientific curiosity and raw grief, full-on noisiness and meditative quiet.

Among her most significant influences is the work of Kevin Quashie, whose publications include The Sovereignty of Quiet: Beyond Resistance in Black Culture, and Black Aliveness, or a Poetics of Being. Prefacing her collection and each of its six sections with resonant epigraphs, Adukwei Bulley takes her first quotation from Quashie: “It is this exploration, this reaction toward the inner life, that an aesthetic of quiet makes possible; and it is this that is the path to a sweet freedom: a black expressiveness without publicness as its forbearer, a black subject in the undisputed dignity of its humanity”. This is relevant to the aims of the collection as a whole, and perhaps particularly to the closing section in which Air appears, headed Night Garden of Yes and Dreaming.

Air has an elusive quality. It leaves things unsaid, slips in and out of metaphor. The opening “Friend” is itself opaque. Friend indicates warmth towards the addressee, and simultaneously respectful distance. The term could define a whole relationship, or an aspect of a relationship – a lover or mother can also be a friend. And of course there’s friendship with oneself, the idea that, for psychological health, the secret self and the public face are in supportive and corrective dialogue. The poet’s sighting of the friend ‘“sitting / at the window of yourself” suggests privacy, the self as non-participant, and the metaphor is developed with the image of the “loft” and the lofty vantage point, “watching it all go on without you”. That’s not to say the images are necessarily non-realistic, but that they’re psychologically true, whatever may be happening physically.

There are further mysteries: ice, shadow, the passing boat. It seems that “ice” denotes transparency rather than coldness – a speaker who can’t be seen, addressing another being who is “almost shadow” – and thus only slightly more visible. The “waving” from the boat may be a cheerful goodbye (a splitting of selves, even). But the goodbye isn’t final: the two figures become “us” in the following stanza. Here, though hesitant, the speaker proposes a further task: “I want us to remember the unreadable air.” In other words, “remember to read the air, here and now”. It’s not an invitation to nostalgia.

One way the air is made readable is through its “recasting” in poetry. In couplets five and six, we’re shown an image of inhalation and exhalation through the paired lines. Breathing, for everyone, becomes “a most humble poem”. Briefly, the peace is broken by an appeal to the addressee, “Don’t go”. But while there’s some tension between encounter and separation in the poem, the focus isn’t primarily a “one-to-one” or “self-to-self” exchange. The poet/speaker pushes the idea of relationship beyond human community: “We must stay alive / to our place in the family // of green & breathing things.”

“What grace,” she exclaims, going on to imagine photosynthesis through her own graceful analogy: these green things “use even our sighs // to make sweetness from light.” The allusion to sweetness and light, qualities first paired by Jonathan Swift, and recast by Matthew Arnold, is nicely judged for its literal and abstract meanings.

Gentle lessons accompany the formal broadening of the couplet to the closing tercet. “Allow what is simple // to be simple. Accept it as truth.” There’s clearly an allowance here for pragmatism: the truth might not be as simple as it looks, but the commitment to truth is necessary to a living and breathing family. A new slant of meaning is introduced in the qualifier of the last sentence, “quiet as it’s kept”. It sheds light on the ethics of the poem and the collection, its art of quietly refusing to keep quiet. The title-word might also suggest a command to readers to “be quiet” and listen.

For all its mystery, the quiet becomes tangible. There’s a beautiful reassurance in the idea that “we help the trees to breathe too” – as if that insight could open possibilities of environmental restoration that constant self-scourging guilt may not always assist.

Adukwei’s spirited and generous work is not always quiet: there are overtly political poems, poems in experimental forms, and a number of spectacularly powerful prose poems, including a long and dazzling riff on “noise”. Buy the collection, but, meanwhile, you can find a discussion of the poet’s background and her film-project Mother Tongues here, as well as some earlier poems at the Poetry Archive.
